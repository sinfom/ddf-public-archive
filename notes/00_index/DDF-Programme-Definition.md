# DDF Programme Definition

## DDF as a Unified Structural Research Programme

The Dual Domain Framework (DDF) provides a mathematically organised route toward a single structural framework linking spacetime kinematics, quantum phase-space structure, spinorial propagation, curvature response, and the role of fundamental constants.

At its present stage, however, DDF should be understood as a **unification programme**, not yet a completed Standard-Model-level theory.

---

## 1. Core Ontological Starting Point

DDF begins from the claim that the observable universe \( U \) is not fundamental, but arises as a projection

$$
P : \Omega \rightarrow U
$$

from a deeper generative domain \( \Omega \).

Observable physics is governed by **projection admissibility**: only those projected configurations that remain spectrally stable, bounded, and coherent persist as physical states.

This provides a single root principle from which multiple physical structures are intended to follow.

---

## 2. Kinematics: Propagation Bound and Lorentzian Structure

A central result of DDF is the **propagation-rigidity chain**.

Admissibility excludes exponential spectral growth and enforces a linear spectral bound, yielding a propagation cone

$$
|\omega| \leq c |k|
$$

whose boundary defines maximal admissible propagation.

Within DDF:

- \( c \) is not introduced as an external constant  
- it is identified as the **slope of the admissible propagation cone**

The associated hyperbolic operator yields the wave equation and establishes emergent Lorentzian kinematics.

---

## 3. First-Order Structure: Dirac Factorisation and Spin

DDF proceeds by factorising the second-order wave operator into a first-order structure.

This introduces:

- a Dirac-type operator  
- a Clifford algebra structure  

From this:

- the Spin group arises naturally as the double cover of Lorentz symmetry  
- spinorial structure is **not postulated**, but emerges from admissible propagation

---

## 4. Quantum Structure: Phase Space and Quantisation

DDF assigns admissible projected states a natural phase-space structure:

- cotangent bundle structure  
- canonical symplectic form  

This leads to:

$$
[X_j, P_k] = i \delta_{jk}
$$

and the Weyl–Heisenberg algebra.

Thus:

- quantisation is not an independent axiom  
- it emerges from the same projection framework that produces finite propagation

However:

- the claim that a **unique universal action scale** \( \hbar \) is forced by topology  
- remains an **open construction problem**

---

## 5. Gravity: Curvature as Projection Response

When projection becomes non-uniform across \( U \), DDF introduces:

- a spin connection  
- covariant derivatives on spinors  

Curvature arises from their commutator.

In this view:

- gravity is not a separate interaction  
- it is the geometric response of the projected domain to non-uniform projection structure  

The key structural identity is:

$$
\mathcal{D}^2 = \nabla^* \nabla + \tfrac{1}{4} R
$$

which links Dirac structure directly to scalar curvature.

---

## 6. Einstein Limit and Newton’s Constant

Applying spectral-action and heat-kernel methods to the DDF Dirac operator yields the Einstein–Hilbert term and:

$$
G = \frac{3\pi}{f_2 \Lambda^2}, 
\quad
G = \frac{3\pi \hbar c}{f_2 \Lambda^2}
$$

This establishes a concrete route linking:

- propagation structure (\( c \))  
- quantum scale (\( \hbar \))  
- gravitational coupling (\( G \))  

Remaining open:

- identifying the spectral cutoff \( \Lambda \) as a **purely internal projection scale**

Until then, gravity is derived **up to a key identification conjecture**.

---

## 7. Constants as a Common Structural Hierarchy

DDF treats physical constants as **structural scales** of the projection architecture.

The current hierarchy is best developed for:

$$
c \rightarrow \hbar \rightarrow G
$$

Other constants:

- \( \alpha \)  
- \( k_B \)  
- \( \Lambda \)  

are presently:

- structurally positioned  
- but not yet quantitatively derived  

Thus, DDF provides a unified conceptual framework for constants, but not yet their numerical determination.

---

## 8. Present Status of the Programme

DDF currently achieves:

- A coherent derivation chain from projection admissibility to:
  - finite propagation  
  - Lorentzian kinematics  
  - Dirac/spin structure  
  - curvature response  
  - an Einstein-like gravity limit  

- A partial construction of quantisation rigidity and \( \hbar \)

However, it does **not yet** provide:

- derivation of the Standard Model gauge group  
- full matter spectrum  
- coupling running or RG structure  
- numerical prediction of physical constants  

---

## 9. Scope and Positioning

DDF should be understood as:

- a **structural unification of frameworks**  
- not yet a full phenomenological unification  

It unifies:

- spacetime structure  
- quantum commutation structure  
- spinorial transport  
- curvature and gravity  
- the conceptual role of constants  

But it does not yet unify:

- all interactions at a predictive level  
- nor reproduce full Standard Model physics  

---

## 10. What DDF Does Not Yet Claim

- No full Standard Model derivation  
- No gauge group or particle content derivation  
- No coupling unification or RG flow  
- No numerical prediction of constants  
- No completed theory of everything  

---

## Summary

DDF is best characterised as:

> A projection-based derivation framework linking propagation bounds, Lorentz/Dirac structure, curvature response, and a developing hierarchy of physical constants.

It is already more than a philosophical model, but remains an **active research programme** rather than a completed unified theory.

## What DDF Does NOT Yet Claim

- No full Standard Model derivation
- No gauge group derivation yet
- No numerical prediction of constants
- No RG flow or coupling unification

DDF is currently a structural unification of frameworks,
not a completed phenomenological theory.
