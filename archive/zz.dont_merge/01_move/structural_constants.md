
# Structural Constants in the Dual‑Domain Framework (DDF)

Status: Foundational definition
Location: 01_foundations/structural_constants.md

---

# 1. Purpose

This document defines how **physical constants are interpreted in the Dual‑Domain Framework (DDF)**.

In conventional physics, constants are treated as unexplained numerical parameters.
In DDF they are **structural parameters of the projection that generates the universe**.

They are therefore **not arbitrary numbers**, but **stability parameters required for a finite projected universe to exist.**

---

# 2. Core Projection Model

The DDF universe is defined by a projection

Ω → U

where

Ω : infinite structure‑rich domain  
U : finite observable universe

The projection operator P compresses Ω into U.

All observable physics arises from the behaviour of the **projection generator L** acting on the universe state ψ.

U evolves according to

(L† L) ψ = M ψ

where

L : projection generator  
ψ : universe state  
M : matrix of structural constants

---

# 3. Meaning of Physical Constants

In DDF, constants are **norms or stability parameters of components of the projection generator L**.

They determine how the projection behaves in different structural directions.

Thus constants are:

• not emergent accidents  
• not tunable parameters of nature  
• not properties of matter

Instead they are **required parameters of the projection process that produces U.**

If these parameters changed, the projection would produce a different universe.

Structural role:

maximum admissible propagation rate of phase under projection

Meaning:

c defines the **propagation cone** of admissible transport.

It is not a property of photons or spacetime but a **stability bound of projection**.

Consequences:

• universal causal structure  
• wave propagation limit  
• Lorentz symmetry as cone‑preserving transformation

---

minimum admissible phase‑space resolution.

Consequences:

• uncertainty relations  
• discrete spectral structure  
• quantum behaviour

projection sensitivity to curvature.

projection entropy resolution scale.

## Cosmological Constant (Λ)

residual projection tension.

# 5. Constraint Surface of Constants

Because all constants originate from the same projection generator L,
they cannot vary independently.

They lie on a shared constraint surface.

Conceptually:

(c² + ħ² + G² + α² + k² + …) = R²

R represents the total intensity of projection.

This explains:

• apparent fine‑tuning of the universe
• stability of physical laws
• absence of independent constant drift

# 6. Interpretation Principle

The DDF interpretation of constants can be summarised as:

Physical constants are structural parameters required for the projection of an infinite domain into a stable finite universe.

They define:

• propagation limits  
• phase‑space resolution  
• curvature coupling  
• interaction strengths  
• entropy scales

Without these parameters the projected universe would not be dynamically stable.

Constants enter the theory through the constant matrix M in the unified equation

(L† L) ψ = M ψ

Each constant corresponds to a structural magnitude of a component of L.

Examples:

spatial transport component → c  
quantisation component → ħ  
curvature component → G  
interaction component → α

Thus physical law emerges from the **geometry of the projection operator itself**.
